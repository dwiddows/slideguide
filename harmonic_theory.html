The Harmonic Series

← Trombone Positions Calculator

The harmonic theory used to explain trombone slide positions can be presented very simply. (The air-pressure mechanics is actually more complicated, see below!)

A tube of a fixed length resonates with standing waves only if an exact number of wave oscillations matches the length of the tube. If there is just one wave oscillation, that is called the fundamental frequency. If there are exactly two waves, the frequency is doubled, and the note is an octave higher (this result is sometimes attributed to Pythagoras though it has been discovered all over the world). Doubling the frequency again gives 4 waves, and the pitch is two octaves higher, and 8 waves is 3 octaves higher. The partials in between give notes within these octaves.

The pitches formed in this way are called the harmonic series of the tube. In 1st position, the notes formed are pedal B♭, then B♭, F, B♭, D, F, A♭ (a well-known flat one, not quite in tune), then B♭ again, three octaves above the fundamental. Detailed Version Using More Realistic Fluid Dynamics

Key mathematical differences between a horn and the simplified wave theory we started with include the following:

  • The air vibrates backwards and forwards, not up and down like on a guitar string.
  • A vibrating string is pinned at both ends: those points can't move at all. A tube isn't closed like that at either end.
    • The bell is open, so the pressure here mixes with the outside air pressure.
    • The pressure at the mouthpiece isn't fixed: pressure changes here are driven by the player's lips.
  • The lips can vibrate at any frequency. (Horns reinforce some frequencies and resist others, which makes it feel like the lips can only make particular notes.)
  • The open end doesn't reflect waves perfectly: some parts escape and make the air around vibrate (otherwise we wouldn't hear anything!)

Without the bell, a plain cylinder, closed at the mouthpiece and open at the bell, only resonates at odd multiples of the fundamental — the even harmonics are simply missing. The bell's gradual flare makes a reflection that lets the even harmonics back in, completing the full series. (If you try removing the bell and playing on just the slide of a trombone, you'll find that there are much bigger gaps between the partials you hear.) This behavior occurs naturally when buzzing into conical animal horns. Centuries of craftsmanship and trial and error led to the design of modern brass instruments, long before the wave dynamics was understood mathematically.

The horn equation for a tube of continuously changing cross-section was formalized by Webster in 1919 (Acoustical Impedance and the Theory of Horns and of the Phonograph, PNAS 5(7):275–282). During the 1920s and 30s horn designers wrestled with these equations for gramophones and telephones (see this brief history of horn acoustics), and the same methods could be used to model brass instruments.

Because reflection and bore shape both vary with frequency, a real tube's partials aren't spaced in the perfectly exact integer ratios an idealized string produces — they have to be measured or modelled one by one, which is what the impedance curve in the experiment below is doing. Solving for this precisely means tracking pressure and airflow together as they move through a tube of continuously changing cross-section (the Webster horn equation), rather than the single simple wave equation that works for a string with fixed ends — a substantially harder mathematical problem.
