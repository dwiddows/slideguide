The Harmonic Series

← Trombone Positions Calculator

The harmonic theory used to explain trombone slide positions can be presented very simply. (The air-pressure mechanics is actually more complicated, see below!)

A tube of a fixed length resonates with standing waves only if an exact number of wave oscillations matches the length of the tube. If there is just one wave oscillation, that is called the fundamental frequency. If there are exactly two waves, the frequency is doubled, and the note is an octave higher (this result is sometimes attributed to Pythagoras though it has been discovered all over the world). Doubling the frequency again gives 4 waves, and the pitch is two octaves higher, and 8 waves is 3 octaves higher. The partials in between give notes within these octaves.

The pitches formed in this way are called the harmonic series of the tube. In 1st position, the notes formed are pedal B♭, then B♭, F, B♭, D, F, A♭ (a well-known flat one, not quite in tune), then B♭ again, three octaves above the fundamental. Every combination of a slide position and a partial number makes a note: trying them all out gives a "trombone notes table" like this:

Note that some notes appear several times once the table is filled out. For example, C4 (middle C) appears at the 5th partial in 3rd position, and the 6th partial in 6th position. It follows that the note C4 can be played on the trombone in either of these positions.

If we follow through this process for all the different notes, we get a related table: for every note, a list of positions where that note can be played on the trombone. This note-to-positions table is shown and used in slide position calculation.

Detailed Version Using More Realistic Fluid Dynamics

The presentation above is really more correct for describing a transverse wave on a string, not a longitudinal pressure wave in a tube! For this, we need a lot more fluid dynamics. The problem of modelling harmonics correctly on a horn wasn't rigourously solved mathematically until the 1930s, and benefited much from developments in gramophone engineering. (Citation needed1)
